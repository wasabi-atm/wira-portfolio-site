Exploring SwiftUI, This is How I Did it as a Designer Learning to Code

“Learning to write programs stretches your mind, and helps you think better, creates a way of thinking about things that I think is helpful in all domains.”
— Bill Gates

As mentioned in my previous article, the ability to code for designers are getting more and more necessary. This is because modern product development is increasingly collaborative and fast-paced — designers who can understand and even write basic code bridge the gap between visuals and functionality.

Designers who understands code are not just handing off static mockups but also contributing to interactive prototypes, catching potential issues early, and even influencing how features are built. In a world where design is expected to be both beautiful and functional, knowing how things work under the hood empowers designers to make smarter, more informed decisions.

In short, a designer who understands the principle of programming will be able to:

  • Collaborate better with developers — by speaking their language and understanding technical constraints, leading to smoother handoffs and fewer revisions.
  • Design with feasibility in mind — knowing what’s possible (and what’s not) helps in creating realistic, efficient, and scalable designs.
  • Prototype smarter — using coded designs more effectively to bring ideas to life with real interactivity.
  • Think in systems — programming encourages logical, modular thinking, which translates into more consistent and scalable design systems.
  • Automate repetitive tasks — saving time with scripts, batch processes, or generative design techniques.
  • Stand out in the industry — blending creativity with technical know-how makes a designer more versatile, valuable, and future-proof.

Basically, coding knowledge sharpens a designer’s problem-solving skills and unlocks creative potential grounded in real-world execution.

Exploring Swift, Native iOS Development

When I decided to dip my toes into coding, I looked for something that felt close to design — visual, intuitive, and not buried in walls of syntax. That’s why I chose to start with SwiftUI.

SwiftUI promised me a more modern, declarative way to build user interfaces — something that made more sense to my designer brain. I wasn’t trying to become a full-time developer, just someone who could bring their ideas to life a little more independently.

And I’ll be honest — jumping into Swift and native iOS development felt intimidating at first. As a designer, I was used to focusing on how things looked and felt, not how they worked behind the scenes.

But…

Once I actually got started, I realized it wasn’t as scary as I thought. Apple’s official documentation is surprisingly beginner-friendly enough, with clear examples and explanations that feel like they’re written for humans, not robots.

On top of that, the “100 Days of SwiftUI” course helped break things down into small, doable chunks. It felt less like climbing a mountain and more like going on a daily walk — and before I knew it, I was building real interfaces and playing with animations that I used to only mock up in Figma.

I started from the first day learning basic programming fundamentals and I was like a baby learning how to crawl. Prior to learning Swift, I have never touched a single line of a programming language, I only have basic understanding of HTML which is just a markdown language.

Slowly but surely, concepts like using variables, constants, states, etc. gradually became easier for me to understand. I started having more confidence and able to visualize how the design will look like in Swift’s 3D stack based layout.

Building My First iOS App with SwiftUI

The first standalone app I try to build was a simple mindfulness app. One that could let users rate their mood, view their emotional trends through graphs, and play calming audio clips — all without getting too deep into complex back-end logic. It felt like the perfect way to learn while building something meaningful.

Working on that app taught me more than just code — it taught me how to think like an iOS developer. I started understanding how data flows, how views update, and how even small user interactions require thoughtful structure.

It wasn’t just about writing Swift; it was about connecting design decisions with real functionality. I learned how to bind mood inputs to state variables, trigger sound playback, and even experiment with Swift Charts to visualize emotional patterns.

What surprised me most was how much control I had. I wasn’t limited by design tools anymore — I could actually build the interactions I had in mind that can hardly be truly replicated in Figma.

That feeling of seeing your design come to life exactly the way you envisioned was game-changing. And suddenly, coding wasn’t this scary, technical thing — it became another creative tool in my toolbox.

Why Designers Shouldn’t be Afraid to Start

If you’re a designer who’s curious but hesitant about learning to code, I totally get it. I was in the same place — worried that it would be too technical, too complicated, or that I’d have to change my identity as a “creative.” But the truth is, coding didn’t take away from my creativity — it expanded it. It gave me more ways to experiment, more freedom to test ideas, and more confidence when collaborating with developers.

One thing I had to constantly remind myself of was not to compare my progress to full-blown developers. It’s easy to feel behind when you see others building complex apps or writing code that looks like a different language entirely (because, well, it is). But I realized — I’m not here to become the next software engineer.

I’m here to understand just enough to bring my ideas to life and work more confidently with developers. That mindset shift made all the difference. Once I stopped putting pressure on myself to “catch up,” I started learning more freely, with less fear and more curiosity.

What’s Next for Me

Now that I’ve gotten a taste of building for iOS, I’m excited to keep pushing my boundaries as a designer-who-codes. I’m currently learning web design with real code — starting by moving my personal portfolio out of Notion and rebuilding it from scratch. It’s a whole new world, but one that’s helping me think more responsively and structure my content with purpose.

I also plan to document my journey exploring more of Apple’s native frameworks — things like NFCKit, Core Motion, and AVFoundation. These are powerful technologies that open up possibilities far beyond what you can prototype in Figma. I want to see how far I can stretch my creativity using hardware and features that are unique to Apple’s ecosystem, and share what I learn with other designers who are curious to do the same.
